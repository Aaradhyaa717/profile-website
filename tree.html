02 / Profiles

Six anti-user profiles

Each persona has a different psychological profile and behavioral fingerprint.

□

The persistent appealer

Confident in innocence

Who they are

  • Believes the ban was triggered by automation or a mistaken report.
  • Convinced they did nothing wrong.
  • Frames the platform decision as automated and correctable.

What they do

  • Sends earnest appeal emails and stays persistent.
  • Files a complaint through BBB if the appeal fails.
  • Believes appeal works for "normal" cases, once it works for them.
  • Often judges people for taking the hacker route.
Outcome: sometimes reinstated if persistent
□

The perpetual victim

Feels wronged, says lost

Who they are

  • Believes the platform or other users acted unfairly.
  • Thinks reporting standards are arbitrary.
  • Thinks the platform are baised against men, are female are too sensitive

What they do

  • Vents on Reddit, TikTok, Instagram, or similar spaces.
  • Looks for people with similar stories.
  • May not actually appeal, remaining stuck in complaint mode.
Outcome: usually stays banned
□

The hacker

Determined to bypass

Who they are

  • Has tried appeals and believes they will not work.
  • Willing to spend money or effort to regain access.
  • Sometimes aware of the behavior that likely triggered the ban.

What they do

  • Uses new phone (Preferably Android) or a secure folder and different Play Store account
  • Uses new phone number, new email, new app store account
  • Avoids using identical pictures (modify them by rotating, screenshotting, add drawings or other modifications in part of the picture that is not visible once uploaded)
  • No personal information or credit card details

    • This can cost somewhere around $100–$150, but it is generally considered to be successful.

    Sites like DatingZest and UnbannedHinge offer services to help with this process.

Outcome: often gets back in, but may face repeated bans
□

The give up-er

Accepts defeat quietly

Who they are

  • May or may not know why they were banned.
  • Does nothing if they know the reason.
  • Tries one appeal, then accepts the outcome.
  • Often females in Instagram and TikTok show this behavior.
  • Considers hard reset as a lot of work to get back in.

What they do

  • Sends maybe one email, then moves on.
  • Migrates to other dating apps or stops using apps entirely.
  • Accepts the ban, might post about it.
Outcome: exits the platform
□

The in-person advocator

Rejects app-first dating

Who they are

  • Gave up after appeals were denied.
  • Uses the ban as a catalyst for change.
  • Often had a mediocre experience before the ban.

What they do

  • Advocates for meeting people in person.
  • Frames the ban as a sign to leave app dating.
  • May post about the shift as a lifestyle change.
Outcome: leaves the app ecosystem
□

The hard reset advocator

Evangelist of the workaround

Who they are

  • Successfully returned after using an evasion pathway.
  • Met people on the platform after re-entry.
  • Now believes appealing is a waste of time.

What they do

  • Promotes workaround narratives on social platforms.
  • Tells newcomers to skip the appeal route.
  • Unmatches frequently, avoids verification, and stays paranoid about a re-ban.
Outcome: back in, but cautious
03 / Tracking

Post-ban signals worth tracking

The downstream behavior matters because a bad appeal experience can reshape the user’s risk appetite after reinstatement, even when the original ban was a false positive.

Reporting others post-reinstatement

Reporting profiles that they have interacted with before through likes, matches, or messages because they assume the other person was responsible for their ban. This can be a sign of a user who is still frustrated and looking for someone to blame.

Unmatching aggressively

Unmatch with people even if the conversation has shifted to another platform and is going well. Also unmatch with people if the initial vibes is off so they can avoid any potential risk of being reported again. This can be a sign of a user who is still paranoid and looking to avoid any potential risk of being banned again.

Avoiding verification

Avoid verification processes to prevent detection and potential re-ban. Avoid in app facial verification, or ID verification done through Jumio

Heavy self-censorship

Users may avoid normal conversation because they fear anything remotely offensive could trigger another ban. The general thought process is that people are "too sensitive" and that they need to be extra careful with their words and actions.
